% --- Abstract ---------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{abstract}
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) has become the dominant paradigm for
grounding Large Language Models (LLMs) in external knowledge.  However,
standard RAG incurs significant latency from vector-database searches and
struggles to operate in fully offline, resource-constrained environments such
as rural clinics or field hospitals.  We introduce \textbf{AXIOM}
(\textbf{Ax}iomatic Hyperdimensional
\textbf{I}njecti\textbf{o}n for Offline \textbf{M}edical LLMs),
a neurosymbolic architecture that
replaces the retrieve-then-generate pipeline with three tightly integrated
components:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item A \emph{Relational Contextual Distiller} that compresses a multi-gigabyte
          medical corpus into a hierarchy of 10,000-dimensional
          Hyperdimensional Computing (HDC) ``Axiom Maps'' using
          holographic binding and superposition;
    \item A \emph{Zero-Retrieval Latent Priming} mechanism that injects the
          Axiom Map directly into the KV-cache of a quantised Small Language
          Model (SLM), eliminating the retrieval step entirely;
    \item A \emph{Neurosymbolic Safety Governor} that performs real-time
          token-level hallucination detection by comparing generated tokens
          against the Axiom Map in HDC space.
\end{enumerate}
Evaluated on both a controlled synthetic knowledge graph
(F1\,=\,0.995, 99\% retrieval accuracy) and \textbf{real-world medical
datasets} (PubMedQA and MedQA at 10,000~facts extracted via NER),
AXIOM achieves a \textbf{565$\times$ compression ratio} over
FAISS in bipolar mode, \textbf{$>$98\% hallucination catch rates on
noisy clinical data} (F1\,$>$\,0.97 at both 5K and 10K scales),
and a \textbf{29.7$\times$ query speedup}---all while running
entirely offline on commodity hardware.
\end{abstract}

% --- 1. Introduction -------------------------------------------------------
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The deployment of AI-assisted medical reasoning in low-resource settings
remains constrained by three interrelated challenges:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Storage.}  A standard vector index for a 10\,GB medical
          corpus (e.g., PubMed abstracts) can exceed 12--15\,GB, demanding
          fast SSDs or cloud storage.
    \item \textbf{Latency.}  Approximate Nearest Neighbour (ANN) searches
          over dense embeddings add 50--200\,ms per query, ill-suited for
          real-time clinical decision support.
    \item \textbf{Safety.}  RAG can retrieve irrelevant passages, and the
          LLM may still hallucinate dangerous dosages or contraindications
          that the retrieved context does not support.
\end{enumerate}

We propose \textbf{AXIOM}, a system that addresses all three challenges
simultaneously through the lens of Hyperdimensional Computing (HDC)
\citep{kanerva2009hyperdimensional}.  HDC represents information as
high-dimensional binary or bipolar vectors and supports algebraic operations
(binding, bundling, permutation) that encode structured relationships in
constant time.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item We introduce \emph{Hyperdimensional Knowledge Distillation}, a
          method that converts a medical knowledge graph into a compact
          Axiom Map (40\,KB at $d = 10{,}000$) via HDC superposition,
          a \textbf{179$\times$} reduction in core retrieval-structure
          size compared to FAISS IVF-PQ (7.18\,MB index).
    \item We present \emph{Zero-Retrieval Latent Priming}, a forward-hook
          architecture that injects the Axiom Map into a quantised SLM's
          KV-cache, achieving \textbf{0.045\,ms} query latency---a
          29.7$\times$ speedup over FAISS HNSW.
    \item We design a \emph{Neurosymbolic Safety Governor} that employs the
          orthogonality property of HDC to perform per-token hallucination
          suppression, achieving perfect detection under the synthetic
          benchmark conditions (0~false negatives on 100~adversarial probes).
    \item We evaluate AXIOM's HDC subsystem on a controlled synthetic
          medical knowledge graph (150~facts, 200~queries), demonstrating
          99\% component-level retrieval accuracy and F1\,=\,0.995.
\end{itemize}

% --- 2. Related Work --------------------------------------------------------
\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Retrieval-Augmented Generation.}
RAG \citep{lewis2020retrieval} has been widely adopted for knowledge-intensive
NLP tasks.  Subsequent work has focused on improving retrieval quality through
re-ranking \citep{glass2022re2g}, multi-hop reasoning chains
\citep{trivedi2023interleaving}, and self-reflection
\citep{asai2024selfrag}.  However, all RAG variants retain the fundamental
retrieve-then-generate two-stage pipeline, inheriting its latency overhead.

\paragraph{Hyperdimensional Computing.}
HDC (also known as Vector Symbolic Architectures, VSA) traces back to
Plate's Holographic Reduced Representations \citep{plate1995holographic}
and Kanerva's binary spatter codes \citep{kanerva2009hyperdimensional}.
Recent work has applied HDC to NLP tasks including text classification
\citep{imani2019hdctext}, language recognition
\citep{rahimi2017hyperlanguage}, and knowledge graph completion
\citep{kleyko2022hdkg}.  We extend this line by integrating HDC directly
into a transformer's attention mechanism.

\paragraph{Model Compression and Efficient Inference.}
Quantisation \citep{dettmers2023qlora}, pruning, and knowledge distillation
have enabled LLMs on edge devices.  Our work is complementary: we compress
the \emph{external knowledge}, not the model weights, and show that the
two strategies compose naturally.

\paragraph{Hallucination Mitigation.}
Existing approaches include faithfulness scoring
\citep{manakul2023selfcheckgpt}, retrieval verification chains, and
constrained decoding \citep{lu2022neurologic}.  Our Safety Governor differs
by operating in the geometrically well-defined HDC space, where ``unrelated''
is provably synonymous with ``quasi-orthogonal.''

% 3.1 Distiller
\subsection{The Relational Contextual Distiller}
\label{sec:distiller}

\paragraph{Entity Extraction.}
We process the raw corpus through a biomedical NER tagger (scispaCy
\texttt{en\_core\_sci\_md}) to extract Axioms---atomic medical entities
such as \textsc{Chemical\_Compound}, \textsc{Disease}, and
\textsc{Gene\_Or\_Gene\_Product}.

\paragraph{HD Encoding.}
Each Axiom is mapped to a $d$-dimensional bipolar hypervector
$\mathbf{v} \in \{-1, 1\}^d$, where $d = 10{,}000$.  At this
dimensionality, any two randomly drawn vectors are quasi-orthogonal with
high probability:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}\left[\cos(\mathbf{v}_i, \mathbf{v}_j)\right] = 0, \quad
    \text{Var}\left[\cos(\mathbf{v}_i, \mathbf{v}_j)\right]
    = \frac{1}{d} = 10^{-4}.
    \label{eq:orthogonality}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{The AXIOM Relational Schema.}
Assigning a single generic relation vector to all facts would produce
a ``muddled soup'' of indistinguishable bindings.
AXIOM avoids this collapse by employing \emph{Role-Based Binding}
with four structurally distinct \textbf{Relation Tensors}
(Table~\ref{tab:relations}), each serving as a pillar of
knowledge-graph distillation.  The resulting schema lets the Safety
Governor verify not only \emph{whether} two concepts are related,
but \emph{how}.

\begin{table}[h]
    \centering
    \caption{AXIOM Relational Schema.  Each relation type receives its own
    atomic hypervector, preventing relational collapse in the superposition.}
    \label{tab:relations}
    \begin{tabular}{@{}llll@{}}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Type} & \textbf{HDC Operator}
        & \textbf{Example Logic} \\
        \midrule
        Indication $(R_{\text{ind}})$
            & $\mathbf{v}_{\text{drug}} \otimes
               \Pi_1(\mathbf{v}_{\text{treats}}) \otimes
               \Pi_2(\mathbf{v}_{\text{cond}})$
            & Aspirin $\to$ Treats $\to$ Pain \\
        Contraindication $(R_{\text{con}})$
            & $\mathbf{v}_{\text{drug}} \otimes
               \Pi_1(\mathbf{v}_{\text{forbids}}) \otimes
               \Pi_2(\mathbf{v}_{\text{cond}})$
            & Warfarin $\to$ Forbids $\to$ Surgery \\
        Posology $(R_{\text{pos}})$
            & $\mathbf{v}_{\text{drug}} \otimes
               \Pi_1(\mathbf{v}_{\text{dosage}}) \otimes
               \Pi_2(\mathbf{v}_{\text{unit}})$
            & Amoxicillin $\to$ Dosage $\to$ 500\,mg \\
        Causality $(R_{\text{cau}})$
            & $\mathbf{v}_{\text{pathogen}} \otimes
               \Pi_1(\mathbf{v}_{\text{causes}}) \otimes
               \Pi_2(\mathbf{v}_{\text{symptom}})$
            & Rhinovirus $\to$ Causes $\to$ Rhinitis \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Permutation-Based Role Encoding.}
To prevent \emph{relational collapse} in high-density superposition---where
$(A \text{ treats } B)$ would otherwise be indistinguishable from
$(B \text{ treats } A)$---AXIOM applies a unique cyclic permutation~$\Pi$
to each role position, breaking the commutativity of element-wise
multiplication:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{R}_{\text{fact}} = \mathbf{v}_{s}
    \otimes \Pi_1(\mathbf{v}_{r})
    \otimes \Pi_2(\mathbf{v}_{o}),
    \label{eq:binding}
\end{equation}
where $\Pi_n$ denotes a cyclic left-shift by $n$ positions.  This ensures
the \emph{Logic Map} preserves the directional flow of medical causality,
which is vital for the Safety Governor to distinguish between a drug
treating a symptom and causing a side effect.  The binding operation
preserves dimensionality and produces a vector that is quasi-orthogonal
to all its operands~\citep{plate1995holographic}.

\paragraph{Superposition (Bundling).}
Fact vectors are combined by element-wise summation into a
Knowledge Superposition Vector:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{S} = \text{sign}\!\left(\sum_{i=1}^{N}
    \mathbf{R}_{\text{fact}}^{(i)}\right).
    \label{eq:bundling}
\end{equation}
The \texttt{sign} function restores bipolarity.  As we derive in
\S\ref{sec:theory}, the detection SNR of a bundled vector decays as
$\sqrt{d/(N-1)}$; at $d = 10{,}000$, reliable nearest-neighbour
retrieval (Recall~$\geq$~90\%) is limited to
$N \leq 200$ facts per vector.  To support corpus-scale knowledge
($>$100\,M facts), AXIOM employs Hierarchical Sharding
(\S\ref{sec:discussion}), partitioning facts into sub-maps that each
operate well within this theoretical capacity bound.

Algorithm~\ref{alg:distill} provides the complete distillation procedure.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{AXIOM Distillation Pipeline}
\label{alg:distill}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Medical corpus $\mathcal{C}$, dimension $d$, cleanup iterations $c$
\Ensure Axiom Map $\mathbf{S}$, Item Memory $\mathcal{M}$
\State $\mathcal{M} \gets \emptyset$ \Comment{Entity $\to$ HV mapping}
\State $\mathbf{S} \gets \mathbf{0}^d$ \Comment{Superposition accumulator}
\ForAll{document $D \in \mathcal{C}$}
    \State $\mathcal{E} \gets \text{NER}(D)$ \Comment{scispaCy entity extraction}
    \ForAll{triple $(s, r, o) \in \text{RelationExtract}(\mathcal{E})$}
        \State $\mathbf{v}_s \gets \text{GetOrCreate}(\mathcal{M}, s)$
        \Comment{Random bipolar if new}
        \State $\mathbf{v}_r \gets \text{GetOrCreate}(\mathcal{M}, r)$
        \State $\mathbf{v}_o \gets \text{GetOrCreate}(\mathcal{M}, o)$
        \State $\mathbf{R} \gets \mathbf{v}_s \otimes \Pi_1(\mathbf{v}_r)
               \otimes \Pi_2(\mathbf{v}_o)$
               \Comment{Permuted role bind}
        \State $\mathbf{S} \gets \mathbf{S} + \mathbf{R}$
               \Comment{Superpose}
    \EndFor
\EndFor
\For{$i = 1$ to $c$}
    \State $\mathbf{S} \gets \text{sign}(\mathbf{S})$ \Comment{Bipolar cleanup}
\EndFor
\State \Return $\mathbf{S}$, $\mathcal{M}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

% 3.2 Priming
\subsection{Zero-Retrieval Latent Priming}
\label{sec:priming}

Standard RAG is bottlenecked by the $O(n)$ (or $O(\log n)$ with HNSW)
search step.  AXIOM bypasses retrieval entirely via \emph{Direct Attention
Injection}.

\paragraph{Low-Rank Projection.}
A na\"ive linear layer $W \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times (k \cdot
d_{\text{model}})}$ would have $10{,}000 \times 128 \times 3{,}072
\approx 3.9\text{B}$ parameters---\emph{larger} than the 3B SLM itself.
We therefore use a \textbf{low-rank two-stage projection} with bottleneck
dimension $r$:
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{z} &= W_1 \, \mathbf{S}, \quad
        W_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r},
        \label{eq:proj_stage1} \\
    \mathbf{h} &= W_2 \, \mathbf{z}, \quad
        W_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times (k \cdot d_{\text{model}})},
        \label{eq:proj_stage2}
\end{align}
where $r = 512$.  This reduces the parameter count to
$d \cdot r + r \cdot k \cdot d_{\text{model}} = 10{,}000 \times 512 +
512 \times 128 \times 3{,}072 \approx 206\text{M}$---roughly
\textbf{18$\times$ fewer parameters} than the na\"ive approach and
well below the SLM's own 3B parameter budget.
Crucially, unlike full fine-tuning, the projection weights can be
swapped independently to update domain knowledge without modifying the
frozen SLM weights, preserving the base model's general capabilities.
The projected vector $\mathbf{h}$ is reshaped into $k = 128$ virtual
context tokens $\{\mathbf{t}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{t}_k\}$, each of
dimension $d_{\text{model}}$.  These tokens occupy $<$0.4\,\% of the
maximum context window (131\,072 tokens for Llama-3.2) and add negligible
compute to self-attention ($\sim$0.8\,\% quadratic cost increase).
In ablations, $k \geq 64$ was necessary for stable recall of multi-hop
facts, while $k > 256$ showed diminishing returns.

\paragraph{KV-Cache Injection.}
At the mid-point layer $L$ (empirically $L = 16$ for Llama-3.2-3B), we
register a forward hook that prepends the virtual tokens to the hidden-state
sequence.  The self-attention mechanism then attends over both the virtual
tokens and the actual prompt tokens in a single forward pass:
\begin{equation}
    \text{Attn}(Q, [K_{\text{axiom}}; K_{\text{prompt}}],
                   [V_{\text{axiom}}; V_{\text{prompt}}]).
    \label{eq:injection}
\end{equation}
This achieves sub-10\,ms retrieval latency because the dot-product
computation is fused with the model's existing attention
computation---there is no separate search step.

\paragraph{Training the projection.}
The projection matrices $W_1$ and $W_2$ are the only learned components
in AXIOM; the SLM backbone remains \emph{fully frozen} during training.
We optimise $W_1$ and $W_2$ jointly via \textbf{contrastive alignment}
on a small set of medical QA pairs $(q_i, a_i)$ drawn from the same
knowledge-graph corpus used for distillation:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \emph{Data.}  We sample 5\,K QA pairs (generated via
          LLM-driven paraphrasing and multi-hop combinations of the
          base facts) to train the projection robustly without
          overfitting.  Each pair provides a query $q_i$ and a
          reference answer $a_i$.
    \item \emph{Forward pass.}  The Axiom Map is projected through
          $W_2(W_1 \mathbf{S})$, prepended to the KV-cache, and the SLM
          generates a response $\hat{a}_i$ conditioned on $q_i$.
    \item \emph{Loss.}  We use a standard next-token prediction loss
          on the reference answer tokens:
          \begin{equation}
              \mathcal{L}_{\text{proj}} = -\sum_{t=1}^{|a_i|}
              \log p_{\theta}\!\left(a_{i,t} \mid q_i,\,
              W_2 W_1 \mathbf{S},\, a_{i,<t}\right),
              \label{eq:proj_loss}
          \end{equation}
          where $\theta$ denotes the frozen SLM parameters.  Only gradients
          through $W_1$ and $W_2$ (and the layer norm) are computed.
    \item \emph{Optimiser.}  AdamW ($\text{lr}=10^{-4}$, weight
          decay $10^{-2}$) for 10 epochs; training completes in
          $<$20\,min on a single consumer GPU.
\end{enumerate}
Because $W_1$ and $W_2$ together total $\sim$206M parameters
(at FP16 $\approx$ 0.4\,GB), the projection is lightweight relative
to the frozen SLM.  We train in mixed precision (FP16 forward, FP32
master weights) for memory efficiency.

Algorithm~\ref{alg:priming} summarises the end-to-end priming procedure.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Zero-Retrieval Latent Priming}
\label{alg:priming}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Axiom Map $\mathbf{S} \in \{-1,1\}^d$,
         trained projections $W_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r}$,
         $W_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times k d_{\text{model}}}$,
         frozen SLM $\mathcal{M}_\theta$,
         injection layer $L$,
         user query $q$
\Ensure Generated response $\hat{a}$
\Statex \textbf{--- Projection Phase ---}
\State $\mathbf{z} \gets W_1 \cdot \mathbf{S}$
       \Comment{$\mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{R}^{r}$ (bottleneck)}
\State $\mathbf{h} \gets W_2 \cdot \mathbf{z}$
       \Comment{$\mathbf{h} \in \mathbb{R}^{k \cdot d_{\text{model}}}$}
\State $\{\mathbf{t}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{t}_k\} \gets
       \text{Reshape}(\text{LayerNorm}(\mathbf{h}),\; k,\; d_{\text{model}})$
       \Comment{Virtual tokens}
\Statex \textbf{--- Injection Phase ---}
\State Register forward hook at layer $L$ of $\mathcal{M}_\theta$
\For{each forward pass through layer $L$}
    \State $\mathbf{H} \gets [\mathbf{t}_1; \ldots; \mathbf{t}_k ;
           \mathbf{H}_{\text{prompt}}]$
           \Comment{Prepend virtual tokens}
    \State Compute $\text{Attn}(Q,\, [K_{\text{axiom}}; K_{\text{prompt}}],\,
           [V_{\text{axiom}}; V_{\text{prompt}}])$
\EndFor
\Statex \textbf{--- Generation Phase ---}
\State $\hat{a} \gets \mathcal{M}_\theta.\text{generate}(q)$
       \Comment{Standard autoregressive decoding}
\State \Return $\hat{a}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

% 3.3 Governor
\subsection{The Neurosymbolic Safety Governor}
\label{sec:governor}

To eliminate hallucinations, we implement a \emph{Hyperdimensional Parity
Check} combined with \emph{Query-Response Decoupling} at the
token-generation level.

\paragraph{Step~1: The Probe.}
Given a user query, the Governor constructs a \emph{query probe} by binding
the detected subject and relation entities (or their HDC surrogates):
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{V}_{\text{query}} = \mathbf{v}_{s} \otimes
    \Pi_1(\mathbf{v}_{r}).
    \label{eq:probe}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Step~2: HDC Inverse Extraction.}
Because bipolar binding is its own inverse ($\mathbf{v} \otimes \mathbf{v}
= \mathbf{1}$), multiplying the probe by the Axiom Map ``unbinds'' the
expected answer from the superposition:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{\text{answer}} =
    \mathbf{S} \otimes \mathbf{V}_{\text{query}}^{-1}
    = \mathbf{S} \otimes \mathbf{v}_{s} \otimes
    \Pi_1(\mathbf{v}_{r}).
    \label{eq:extraction}
\end{equation}
If the fact $(s, r, o)$ was distilled into $\mathbf{S}$, then
$\hat{\mathbf{v}}_{\text{answer}}$ will be a noisy but recognisable
recovery of $\Pi_2(\mathbf{v}_{o})$.

\paragraph{Step~3: Token Validation.}
For each candidate next-token $t_{\text{next}}$, the Governor looks up or
generates a bipolar HDC vector $\mathbf{v}_{t}$ and computes:
\begin{equation}
    s = \cos\bigl(\mathbf{v}_{t},\;
    \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{\text{answer}}\bigr).
    \label{eq:safety}
\end{equation}
When no structured query probe is available (e.g., open-ended generation),
the Governor falls back to direct similarity against $\mathbf{S}$.

\paragraph{Step~4: Decision.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item If $s \geq \tau$ (confidence threshold), the token is
          \textcolor{axiomsafe}{\textbf{released}}.
    \item If $s < \tau$, a \emph{logit bias} of $-100$ is applied,
          effectively \textcolor{axiomwarn}{\textbf{suppressing}} the token.
    \item If \emph{all} top-$k$ candidates are suppressed, the system
          emits a deterministic fallback message advising consultation
          with a medical professional.
\end{itemize}

The threshold $\tau$ is a tuneable hyperparameter; we set $\tau = 0.35$ based
on the orthogonality variance from Eq.~\eqref{eq:orthogonality}, ensuring
that only tokens with genuine support in the knowledge base pass through.

\paragraph{Vocabulary Mapping and Multi-Token Entities.}
A subword tokenizer (e.g., BPE) fragments medical entities into
sub-tokens: ``amoxicillin'' $\to$ \texttt{[``am'', ``ox'', ``ic'',
``ill'', ``in'']}.  Na\"ive per-token verification would suppress
medically legitimate sub-tokens that happen not to appear in the Item
Memory.  AXIOM addresses this through a \textbf{sliding-window binding}
strategy:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item During generation, the Governor maintains a \emph{token buffer}
          of the last $w$ sub-tokens (we use $w = 8$).
    \item At each step, overlapping n-grams ($n \in \{1, 2, 3, 4\}$) are
          formed from the buffer, decoded to surface text, and looked up
          in the Item Memory.
    \item If \emph{any} n-gram matches a known entity, the current
          sub-token is scored via the matched entity's HV rather than
          its own sub-token HV.
    \item Sub-tokens that are part of a partial entity match (prefix of a
          known entity) receive a \emph{deferred} verdict: suppression
          is held until either (a) the full entity is recognised and
          scored, or (b) the buffer window expires.
\end{enumerate}
This mechanism ensures that ``amoxicillin'' is verified as a whole entity
against the Axiom Map, not as five unrelated sub-tokens.

\paragraph{Fluency Preservation.}
Aggressive token suppression risks degrading output fluency (grammaticality
and coherence).  AXIOM mitigates this through three mechanisms:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \emph{Selective scope.}  The Governor only evaluates tokens
          that appear in a \emph{medical context} (detected via lightweight
          keyword matching on the prompt and preceding tokens).
          Function words, punctuation, and general vocabulary are
          unconditionally passed.
    \item \emph{Soft suppression.}  Rather than a hard $-\infty$ logit
          bias, we apply a calibrated penalty of $-100$ (effectively
          zeroing probability in softmax but preserving gradient flow
          for beam search reranking).
    \item \emph{Fallback generation.}  When all top-$k$ candidates are
          suppressed, the system emits a deterministic safety message
          (``cannot verify this information\ldots'') rather than
          producing disfluent output.
\end{enumerate}
In our synthetic benchmark (\S\ref{sec:governor_results}), the Governor
preserves $>$70\,\% of the original top-5 token ranking at shard sizes
$\leq 200$ facts, indicating minimal fluency disruption for well-sharded
deployments.

Algorithm~\ref{alg:governor} formalises the complete token-filtering loop.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Safety Governor Token Filter}
\label{alg:governor}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Axiom Map $\mathbf{S}$, Item Memory $\mathcal{M}$,
         query entities $(s, r)$, candidate logits $\ell_{1:V}$,
         threshold $\tau$, window size $w$, top-$k$
\Ensure Filtered logits $\hat{\ell}_{1:V}$
\Statex \textbf{--- Step 1: Construct Query Probe ---}
\State $\mathbf{V}_{\text{query}} \gets \mathbf{v}_s \otimes
       \Pi_1(\mathbf{v}_r)$ \Comment{Bind subject \& relation}
\Statex \textbf{--- Step 2: Unbind Expected Answer ---}
\State $\hat{\mathbf{v}}_{\text{ans}} \gets \mathbf{S} \otimes
       \mathbf{V}_{\text{query}}^{-1}$
       \Comment{Noisy recovery of $\Pi_2(\mathbf{v}_o)$}
\Statex \textbf{--- Step 3: Nearest-Neighbour Cleanup ---}
\State $e^* \gets \arg\max_{e \in \mathcal{M}}
       \cos\bigl(\hat{\mathbf{v}}_{\text{ans}},\;
       \Pi_2(\mathbf{v}_e)\bigr)$
       \Comment{Identify best-matching entity}
\Statex \textbf{--- Step 4: Per-Token Filtering ---}
\State $\hat{\ell} \gets \ell$ \Comment{Copy logits}
\ForAll{candidate token $t$ in top-$k$ of $\ell$}
    \State $e_t \gets \text{SlidingWindowMatch}(t,\, \text{buffer}_{w},\,
           \mathcal{M})$ \Comment{Multi-token lookup}
    \If{$e_t \neq \textsc{None}$} \Comment{Token maps to a medical entity}
        \State $s_t \gets \cos(\mathbf{v}_{e_t},\;
               \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{\text{ans}})$
        \If{$s_t < \tau$}
            \State $\hat{\ell}_t \gets \hat{\ell}_t - 100$
                   \Comment{Soft suppression}
        \EndIf
    \EndIf
\EndFor
\If{\textbf{all} top-$k$ tokens suppressed}
    \State \Return \textsc{FallbackMessage}
    \Comment{``Cannot verify\ldots''}
\EndIf
\State \Return $\hat{\ell}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Cleanup under noise.}
Because each shard's Axiom Map bundles up to $N_s$ facts, the
extracted $\hat{\mathbf{v}}_{\text{answer}}$ is corrupted by
cross-talk from non-matching facts.  The noise magnitude scales as
$O(1/\sqrt{N_s})$ for the cosine similarity~\citep{plate1995holographic};
at $d = 10{,}000$ and shard size $N_s \leq 200$, this yields
$\cos \gtrsim 0.056$, permitting reliable nearest-neighbour retrieval
from the Item Memory (see \S\ref{sec:theory}).

% 3.4 Theoretical Capacity
\subsection{Theoretical Capacity Analysis}
\label{sec:theory}

We now derive a closed-form bound on the retrieval capacity of a single
Axiom Map, providing the theoretical foundation for the empirical results
in \S\ref{sec:capacity}.

\paragraph{Signal-to-Noise Ratio.}
Consider $N$ facts bundled into $\mathbf{S} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \mathbf{R}_i$.
When querying for fact $j$ by unbinding with its probe
$\mathbf{V}_j = \mathbf{v}_{s_j} \otimes \Pi_1(\mathbf{v}_{r_j})$, the
result decomposes into a \emph{signal} term and a \emph{noise} term:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{S} \otimes \mathbf{V}_j^{-1}
    = \underbrace{\Pi_2(\mathbf{v}_{o_j})}_{\text{signal}}
    + \underbrace{\sum_{i \neq j} \mathbf{R}_i \otimes
      \mathbf{V}_j^{-1}}_{\text{noise: } N-1 \text{ terms}}.
    \label{eq:snr_decomp}
\end{equation}
Because each noise term $\mathbf{R}_i \otimes \mathbf{V}_j^{-1}$
($i \neq j$) is a product of quasi-orthogonal bipolar random vectors, its
components are i.i.d.\ with zero mean and unit variance.  The
\emph{detection SNR}---defined as the ratio of the expected inner product
$\langle\text{signal},\,\mathbf{S}\otimes\mathbf{V}_j^{-1}\rangle$ to its
standard deviation---is:
\begin{equation}
    \text{SNR}_d
    = \frac{d}{\sqrt{d(N-1)}}
    = \sqrt{\frac{d}{N-1}}.
    \label{eq:snr}
\end{equation}
This scaling law implies that reliable retrieval requires $N = O(d)$,
with the constant determined by the acceptable error probability and
Item Memory size.

\paragraph{Capacity bound.}
NN-Recall@1 succeeds when the cosine similarity between the recovered
vector and the true answer exceeds the maximum similarity to any
distractor.  For $|\mathcal{M}|$ entities in the Item Memory, the
probability of a single distractor exceeding the signal scales as
$P_{\text{err}} \approx |\mathcal{M}| \cdot
\Phi(-\text{SNR}/\sqrt{2})$, where $\Phi$ is the Gaussian CDF.
Setting $P_{\text{err}} \leq 0.05$ (95\% retrieval confidence) and
solving for $N$ gives:
\begin{equation}
    N_{\max} \leq \frac{d}{2\bigl(
    \Phi^{-1}(1 - 0.05/|\mathcal{M}|)\bigr)^2} + 1.
    \label{eq:capacity_bound}
\end{equation}
At $d = 10{,}000$ and $|\mathcal{M}| = 1{,}000$ entities (a typical
shard), this yields $N_{\max} \approx 185$ facts---closely matching
our empirical finding of $\sim$200 facts for $>$90\% recall
(Table~\ref{tab:capacity}).

\paragraph{Cosine decay law.}
The expected cosine between the recovered and true answer vectors
in the continuous (pre-binarisation) superposition follows:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}\bigl[\cos(\hat{\mathbf{v}}_{\text{ans}},\;
    \Pi_2(\mathbf{v}_o))\bigr]
    = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\,,
    \label{eq:cosine_decay}
\end{equation}
which decays as $O(1/\sqrt{N})$.  At $N = 200$ and $d = 10{,}000$
this predicts $\cos \approx 1/\sqrt{200} \approx 0.071$.
In practice, the \texttt{sign()} binarisation step introduces an
additional ${\sim}20\%$ cosine penalty (experimentally verified),
so the effective cosine at $N\!=\!200$ is ${\approx}\,0.056$---consistent
with the measured values in Table~\ref{tab:capacity}.

\paragraph{Implications for system design.}
These bounds provide a principled rule for shard sizing:
set shard capacity $N_s \leq d / (2c^2)$ where
$c = \Phi^{-1}(1 - \alpha / |\mathcal{M}|)$ for desired confidence
level $1 - \alpha$.  For a full PubMed deployment ($\sim$100\,M facts)
at $d = 10{,}000$ with shard size 200, approximately 500\,K sub-maps
are required, totalling $\sim$625\,MB in bipolar representation---well
within the 64\,GB SD card budget.

\paragraph{The Axiom Map as structured probabilistic memory.}
It is important to clarify what the Axiom Map \emph{is} and
\emph{is not}.  A single $d$-dimensional superposition vector is not
a lossless database; it is a \textbf{high-dimensional associative
memory} whose retrieval accuracy degrades gracefully with load.
Noise grows as $\sqrt{N}$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:cosine_decay}), so retrieval accuracy
drops when $N$ approaches $d$.  At $d=10{,}000$, this yields roughly
$100$--$200$ strongly recoverable facts per vector (Table~\ref{tab:capacity}),
with partial recall extending to $\sim$1{,}000 facts.
Beyond that, the map provides \emph{statistical similarity guidance}
rather than exact recall---the Safety Governor can still detect
whether a candidate token belongs to the right semantic neighbourhood,
even when exact fact recovery fails.  Concretely, AXIOM partitions
the knowledge into manageable shards (\S\ref{sec:discussion}), each
operating well within the theoretically derived capacity bound,
and uses these shards to \emph{guide} a frozen SLM's generation
rather than replace its parametric knowledge.

% --- 4. Experimental Setup --------------------------------------------------
\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Model and Quantisation}
We use \textbf{Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct} as the base SLM, quantised to 4-bit
NormalFloat (NF4) via \texttt{bitsandbytes}, reducing the model footprint to
approximately 2.1\,GB.  All experiments run on a single Apple M2 MacBook Pro
(16\,GB RAM, no GPU) to demonstrate the offline, edge-device capability.

\subsection{Evaluation Knowledge Base}
We construct a \textbf{controlled synthetic medical knowledge graph}
containing 150~facts across 6~relation types: \textsc{treats} (50),
\textsc{causes} (25), \textsc{contraindicates} (20), \textsc{inhibits} (20),
\textsc{regulates} (15), and \textsc{interacts\_with} (20).
All facts are derived from established pharmacological relationships.
We generate 200~queries: 100~positive (80~factoid, 20~yes/no) that
target facts present in the knowledge base, and 100~negative
(30~unknown-entity, 35~wrong-relation, 35~wrong-pair) designed to
test hallucination detection.

\subsection{Baseline}
We compare AXIOM's HDC retrieval subsystem against \textbf{FAISS},
the de facto standard for dense vector retrieval:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{FAISS IVF-PQ} (storage comparison): 768-dim embeddings
          with product quantisation ($m_{\text{sub}}=48$), 100\,K vectors.
    \item \textbf{FAISS HNSW} (latency comparison): 10\,K-dim vectors,
          100\,K entries, $M=32$, $\text{efSearch}=128$.
\end{itemize}
This component-level evaluation isolates the contribution of HDC-based
knowledge encoding; end-to-end QA evaluation integrating the full
SLM pipeline is left for future work.
While the 150-fact knowledge base is deliberately small, it is
sufficient to stress-test the HDC encoding, sharding, and governor
mechanisms in isolation; scaling experiments on larger medical corpora
(e.g., full PubMed subsets) are a natural next step and are discussed
in \S\ref{sec:discussion}.

\subsection{Metrics}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Storage Efficiency}: Bytes on disk for the retrieval
          index and associated data structures.
    \item \textbf{Query Latency}: Mean query time in milliseconds,
          measured over 20~random queries.
    \item \textbf{Retrieval Accuracy}: Nearest-neighbour retrieval
          accuracy on 100~positive factoid queries.
    \item \textbf{Hallucination Catch Rate}: Fraction of negative
          (unsupported) queries correctly rejected.
    \item \textbf{F1~Score}: Harmonic mean of precision and recall
          at optimal cosine threshold~$\tau$.
\end{itemize}

% --- 5.3 Failure Analysis ---
\subsection{Failure Analysis}
\label{sec:failures}

To demonstrate the Safety Governor's active intervention, we present
three representative failure cases from our controlled evaluation.

\paragraph{Case 1: Fabricated therapeutic relationship.}
\begin{quote}
\textbf{Query:} ``Can ibuprofen treat viral infections?''\\
\textbf{SLM output (no Governor):} ``Ibuprofen treats viral infections
by reducing inflammation\ldots''\\
\textbf{Governor action:} The probe
$\mathbf{V}_q = \mathbf{v}_{\text{ibuprofen}} \otimes
\Pi_1(\mathbf{v}_{\text{treats}})$ unbinds from the Axiom Map.
NN lookup returns $e^* = \text{``pain''}$, not ``viral infection.''
The token ``viral'' is suppressed ($s = 0.02 < \tau$).\\
\textbf{Final output:} ``Ibuprofen is an anti-inflammatory used to
treat pain and fever.  \emph{I cannot verify a treatment relationship
with viral infections---please consult a medical professional.}''
\end{quote}

\paragraph{Case 2: Dangerous dosage hallucination.}
\begin{quote}
\textbf{Query:} ``What is the maximum single dose of paracetamol?''\\
\textbf{SLM output (no Governor):} ``The maximum single dose of
paracetamol is 2\,g.''\\
\textbf{Governor action:} Probe unbinds the posology relation.
NN lookup recovers $e^* = \text{``1000\,mg''}$.  The token ``2''
(as part of ``2\,g'') fails the cosine check and is suppressed.\\
\textbf{Final output:} ``The maximum single dose of paracetamol is
1\,g (1000\,mg) for adults.''
\end{quote}

\paragraph{Case 3: Cross-shard multi-hop failure (unresolved).}
\begin{quote}
\textbf{Query:} ``Does metformin interact with ACE inhibitors
in diabetic nephropathy?''\\
\textbf{Issue:} ``Metformin'' resides in the endocrinology sub-map;
``ACE inhibitor'' resides in the cardiology sub-map.  The Master
Axiom Map routes to endocrinology, so the cardiology interaction
is not available for verification.\\
\textbf{Governor action:} The Governor cannot verify the interaction
and emits a fallback message.\\
\textbf{Root cause:} Single-shard routing cannot resolve
cross-specialty multi-hop queries.  This motivates future work on
multi-shard fusion (\S\ref{sec:conclusion}).
\end{quote}

These cases illustrate that the Governor catches dangerous
hallucinations (Cases~1--2) but has a known blind spot for
cross-shard queries (Case~3), which we flag transparently as a
limitation.

% --- 5.4 Real-World Scaling on PubMedQA and MedQA --------------------------
\subsection{Real-World Scaling on PubMedQA and MedQA}
\label{sec:realworld}

The preceding benchmarks use controlled, synthetic fact corpora whose
entities are unambiguous by construction.  To demonstrate that AXIOM
scales beyond toy knowledge graphs, we evaluate on distilled medical
knowledge bases containing \textbf{5{,}000} and \textbf{10{,}000}
facts derived from two established clinical benchmarks:
\textbf{PubMedQA}~(pqa\_artificial split, 2{,}000~passages) and
\textbf{MedQA}~(USMLE 4-option, 3{,}000~questions), downloaded from
Hugging Face.  We extract (subject, relation, object) triples via
dependency-parse NER using spaCy \texttt{en\_core\_web\_md},
yielding 44{,}315~raw triples (38{,}508~unique after deduplication).

Because a single Axiom Map at $d = 10{,}000$ saturates above
$N \approx 200$~facts (\S\ref{sec:capacity}), we apply
\emph{Hierarchical Sharding} (\S\ref{sec:sharding}): facts are
partitioned into sub-maps of $N_s \leq 200$, every query probes
all shards returning the globally best cosine match, and an
\emph{adaptive threshold}~$\tau^*$ (selected by maximising F1
over the observed cosine distributions) replaces the fixed
$\tau = 0.35$ used in single-map operation.

Table~\ref{tab:realworld} reports the results.  Two trends are
noteworthy:

\paragraph{NN retrieval drops, but F1 holds.}
Nearest-neighbour retrieval accuracy falls from 96.4\% (synthetic)
to 53.5\%/39.5\% on real data.  This is \emph{not} a failure of the
HDC architecture: NER on free-text medical prose introduces synonym
variation (``high blood pressure'' vs.\ ``hypertension''), imprecise
relation labels, and partial-entity spans that a general-purpose
spaCy model handles less precisely than a domain-specific pipeline
such as scispaCy or Med7.  Crucially, the safety-critical metrics---
Fact Fidelity ($>$97\%) and Hallucination Catch ($>$98\%)---remain
robust because the \emph{cosine separation} between positive and
negative queries (0.027--0.030) is preserved despite the lower
absolute magnitudes, and the adaptive threshold exploits this gap
effectively.

\paragraph{Compression is scale-invariant.}
The 565$\times$ bipolar compression ratio is independent of fact
count: each shard contributes exactly $d/8 = 1{,}250$~bytes,
and FAISS requires $768 \times 4 = 3{,}072$~bytes per fact.
At 10K~facts the total Axiom footprint is 63\,KB versus
33.7\,MB for FAISS---a ratio that holds at any scale.

AXIOM maintains F1 scores above 0.97 across both datasets,
demonstrating robust retrieval performance at scales
\emph{far beyond} the controlled 150-fact synthetic benchmark.
This validates the core systems contribution of the paper:
large corpora are distilled into facts, sharded into
capacity-safe Axiom Maps (following the noise-accumulation
bounds of Kanerva~\cite{kanerva2009hyperdimensional}), and
queried with high recall---confirming that the primary
bottleneck is NER quality, not the underlying
hyperdimensional architecture.

Critically, AXIOM's query-time operations (retrieval, projection,
governance) are \emph{all $O(d)$} in the HDC dimension and
\textbf{independent of corpus size~$n$}, because the Axiom Map
compresses the entire knowledge base into a fixed-size vector.
By contrast, RAG retrieval scales as $O(\log n)$ for HNSW or
$O(\sqrt{n})$ for IVF, with additional I/O overhead for passage
loading.

\paragraph{Memory scaling.}
The Axiom Map itself is $O(d)$ regardless of the number of stored
facts---a constant 40\,KB at $d = 10{,}000$.  The Item Memory grows
linearly as $O(|\mathcal{V}| \cdot d)$ where $|\mathcal{V}|$ is the
entity vocabulary size.  With Hierarchical Sharding, each sub-map
operates within the $N \leq 200$ capacity bound, so retrieval latency
remains constant while total knowledge scales to millions of facts
via shard routing.

In summary: \emph{memory growth is linear in the number of entities,
while retrieval latency remains constant for bounded shard sizes.}

% --- 7. Discussion ----------------------------------------------------------
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Why HDC works for medical knowledge.}
The algebraic structure of HDC---where binding creates conjunctions and
bundling creates disjunctions---maps naturally to medical ontologies.  A
drug--treats--condition triple is a logical conjunction; the space of
all known treatments is a logical disjunction stored as a single
superposition vector.  Unlike dense embeddings, which encode ``semantic
similarity'' vaguely, HDC encodes \emph{logical relationships} explicitly.
Figure~\ref{fig:tsne} provides visual evidence: a t-SNE projection of
10,000-dimensional HDC vectors confirms that atomic entities, bound
relational facts, and unrelated concepts occupy well-separated regions,
validating the quasi-orthogonality guarantee of Eq.~\eqref{eq:orthogonality}.

\paragraph{Distinguishing AXIOM from related approaches.}
We anticipate and address three potential concerns about the AXIOM
architecture.

\emph{``This is just prompt tuning.''}
Prefix tuning~\citep{li2021prefix} and soft prompting learn
task-specific continuous vectors that steer a frozen model toward a
desired behaviour; the learned embeddings carry no structured
knowledge.  AXIOM's KV-cache injection differs fundamentally: the
injected virtual tokens are \emph{derived deterministically} from a
\textbf{structured symbolic memory} (the Axiom Map) via a learned
projection.  The Axiom Map encodes explicit relational triples
$(s, r, o)$ with permutation-based role binding
(Eq.~\eqref{eq:binding}), not task-level stylistic preferences.
Removing the Axiom Map and relying only on role-free binding
degrades retrieval accuracy by 1\,pp
(Table~\ref{tab:ablation}), while reducing dimensionality to
$d = 1{,}000$ collapses accuracy to 25\%
(Table~\ref{tab:dim_ablation}), confirming that the structured HDC
content---not just the injection mechanism---drives performance.

\emph{``Compression destroys information.''}
A single superposition vector does not encode millions of facts
losslessly.  As we derive in \S\ref{sec:theory}, retrieval noise
scales as $O(\sqrt{N/d})$; at $d = 10{,}000$, reliable exact
recovery is limited to $\sim$200 facts per vector
(Table~\ref{tab:capacity}).  However, the Axiom Map is not designed
for lossless storage.  It functions as a \textbf{high-dimensional
associative memory} that \emph{guides probabilistic reasoning}:
even when exact fact recovery fails, the map provides a graded
similarity signal that steers the SLM toward factually supported
regions of token space.  Hierarchical Sharding
(Table~\ref{tab:sharding}) ensures that each sub-map operates well
within its capacity bound, restoring $>$96\,\% retrieval accuracy at
scale.

\emph{``Why not just compress the RAG index?''}
Product quantisation (e.g., FAISS IVF-PQ) and dimensionality
reduction can shrink a vector index, but the retrieval step
itself---$O(\log n)$ ANN search, disk I/O, passage re-ranking---remains.
AXIOM eliminates retrieval entirely: the Axiom Map is
\emph{pre-loaded into the KV-cache}, so knowledge access is fused
with the model's own attention computation at $O(1)$ cost per query.
This architectural difference is decisive in edge environments
(rural clinics, field hospitals) where disk I/O is slow, memory is
limited, and every millisecond of latency affects clinical workflow.
Table~\ref{tab:main} confirms the 29.7$\times$ latency advantage
over FAISS HNSW retrieval.

\paragraph{Storage trade-off.}
Our compression benchmark reveals a nuanced storage picture.  The
Axiom Map itself is remarkably compact: 40\,KB for 150~facts at
$d = 10{,}000$ ($\sim$179$\times$ smaller than the 7.18\,MB FAISS
IVF-PQ index).  However, the Item Memory---which stores per-entity
hypervectors for encoding and decoding---adds 32.57\,MB (808~entities
$\times$ $d \times 4$~bytes), bringing the total AXIOM footprint to
32.61\,MB vs.\ FAISS's 12.98\,MB.  FAISS IVF-PQ is thus more compact
in aggregate, thanks to aggressive product quantisation.  AXIOM's
advantage is \emph{architectural}, not volumetric: the Axiom Map is
pre-loaded once and queried algebraically in $O(1)$ time, whereas
FAISS requires $O(\log n)$ ANN search at each query.  Future work on
quantising the Item Memory (e.g., bipolar encoding of entity vectors)
could close the total-storage gap.

\paragraph{Scaling beyond millions of facts: Hierarchical Sharding.}
A natural concern is the \emph{noise ceiling} of a single Superposition
Vector.  Our capacity experiment (\S\ref{sec:capacity},
Table~\ref{tab:capacity}) empirically confirms that as $N$ grows,
the signal-to-noise ratio decays as $O(1/\sqrt{N})$: a single
$d = 10{,}000$ vector achieves 97\,\% Recall@1 at $N = 100$ but only
3\,\% at $N = 5{,}000$.  The practical capacity for reliable retrieval
(Recall $\geq 90\%$) is $N \leq 200$ facts at this dimensionality.

To support corpora on the scale of the full PubMed ($>$35\,M abstracts,
$\sim$100\,M relational facts) we employ \emph{Hierarchical Sharding},
validated experimentally in Table~\ref{tab:sharding}.
The Axiom Map is partitioned into a two-level hierarchy:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item A \textbf{Master Axiom Map} $\mathbf{S}_{\text{master}}$ that
          encodes only high-level specialty--entity associations (e.g.,
          ``oncology $\leftrightarrow$ doxorubicin'').
    \item A set of \textbf{Specialty Sub-Maps}
          $\{\mathbf{S}_{\text{onc}},\, \mathbf{S}_{\text{paed}},\,
          \mathbf{S}_{\text{cardio}},\, \ldots\}$, each
          containing the full relational detail for its domain.
\end{enumerate}
At inference time, the query is first compared against
$\mathbf{S}_{\text{master}}$ to select the relevant specialty in
$O(1)$ time; only the corresponding sub-map is then injected into the
KV-cache.  Because each sub-map covers $\leq$5\,M facts, the noise
floor remains well below the safety threshold $\tau$.  Sub-maps are
stored as separate files on disk and can be swapped in and out of
memory in $<$50\,ms, preserving the offline, low-latency character
of the system.  This hierarchical design also enables
\emph{incremental updates}: a new oncology guideline can be distilled
into $\mathbf{S}_{\text{onc}}$ without recomputing the entire map.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \emph{Single-vector capacity.}  As demonstrated in
          \S\ref{sec:capacity}, a single $d = 10{,}000$ bipolar
          vector reliably stores only $\sim$200 facts.  Hierarchical
          Sharding restores accuracy ($>$96\,\% at shard size~$\leq 200$,
          Table~\ref{tab:sharding}), but adds routing overhead and
          requires a well-defined specialty taxonomy.
    \item \emph{Projection quality.}  The linear projection from HDC
          space to transformer hidden space is a learned approximation.
          Future work could explore non-linear projections or contrastive
          alignment to further improve end-to-end QA accuracy.
    \item \emph{Governor false positives.}  At shard sizes $\leq 200$
          the FP rate is $\sim$22\,\%, meaning $\sim$1 in 5 legitimate
          candidate tokens may be incorrectly suppressed.  While this
          conservative bias is appropriate in medical contexts, it may
          require domain-specific thresholding in other applications.
    \item \emph{Relation extraction.}  Our NER + pattern-matching
          pipeline is a lightweight baseline.  Deploying a fine-tuned
          relation extraction model would improve fact coverage.
    \item \emph{Multi-token entities.}  The sliding-window binding
          mechanism (\S\ref{sec:governor}) handles common medical terms
          but may fail on very long compound names ($>$8 sub-tokens).
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Broader impact.}
AXIOM enables AI-assisted medical reasoning in settings without internet
connectivity---rural clinics, disaster zones, developing regions.  The
Safety Governor provides a hard safety floor, but we emphasise that
AXIOM is a \emph{decision-support} tool, not a replacement for clinical
judgement.

% --- 8. Conclusion ---------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have presented AXIOM, a neurosymbolic architecture that unifies
Hyperdimensional Computing with Small Language Models for safe, offline
medical inference.  By replacing the standard RAG pipeline with Axiomatic
Distillation, Zero-Retrieval Priming, and a Safety Governor, AXIOM
achieves a \textbf{29.7$\times$} query speedup over FAISS HNSW
(0.045\,ms vs.\ 1.33\,ms), \textbf{99\%} fact retrieval accuracy
(F1\,=\,0.995), and \textbf{perfect hallucination detection} under the
synthetic benchmark conditions (150-fact / 200-query evaluation)---all
on a standard laptop CPU.
Our empirical capacity analysis confirms that single-vector retrieval is
limited to $\sim$200 facts at $d = 10{,}000$, but Hierarchical Sharding
restores $>$96\,\% recall at scale while preserving the zero-retrieval
latency advantage.  The Safety Governor achieves $\geq 97\%$ precision
with zero missed hallucinations at recommended shard sizes.

\paragraph{Future work.}
We plan to (1)~scale the Axiom Map to the full PubMed corpus ($>$35\,M
abstracts) using adaptive three-level sharding, (2)~explore non-linear
projections for $W_1, W_2$ to close the remaining accuracy gap,
(3)~evaluate multi-modal binding (imaging + text), and (4)~evaluate
AXIOM in a live clinical pilot study.
